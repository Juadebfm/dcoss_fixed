What is business process re-engineering (BPR)?

Business process reengineering (BPR) is the radical redesign of business processes to achieve dramatic improvements in critical aspects such as quality, output, cost, service, and speed. Business process reengineering (BPR) aims to drastically reduce enterprise costs and process redundancies. BPR initially resembles business process improvement (BPI). However, the two are distinct due to some fundamental variances. BPI may involve a few minor rule changes here and there. However, reengineering is an unrestricted method for seeing past the boundaries that have been set and introducing seismic modifications. BPR considers the bigger picture, in contrast to BPI, which is an incremental setup that focuses on tweaking current processes in order to improve them.

Reengineering business processes in five steps (BPR)

          To keep business process reengineering fair, transparent, and efficient, stakeholders must gain a better understanding of the key steps involved. Although the process varies from one organization to the next, the steps listed below summarize the process: The following are the five steps for Business Process Re-engineering:

1. Develop a map of the actual position and current state of your business processes.

Collect data from all sources, including software tools and stakeholders. Determine how the process is currently performing.

2. Analyze them to find flaws or disconnects in the process.

Pinpoint all errors and delays that are adversely affecting the free flow of the procedure. Check to see if all details are available in the appropriate steps for stakeholders to make quick decisions.

3. Find areas for improvement and validate them.

Verify if each action is truly necessary. Remove a step if its main purpose is to inform the person, and replace it with an automatic email trigger.

4. Create an innovative future-state process map.

Create a new procedure that addresses all of the issues you've identified. Do n't be afraid to invent a new procedure that will undoubtedly be successful. Establish key performance indicators (KPIs) for each stage of the procedure.

5. Implement future state modifications while keeping dependencies in mind.

Notify all stakeholders about the new process. Move ahead only after everyone is on board and understands how the new process works. Monitor the KPIs at all times.

When should you consider business process re-engineering (BPR) ?

The issue with BPR is that it is more expensive to implement the larger you are. Five months after launch, a startup may undergo a pivot that includes business process reengineering with minimal execution costs. However, as an organization grows, it will find it more difficult and costly to completely reengineer its processes. They are, however, the ones who are forced to change as a result of competition and unexpected market shifts. The demand for BPR is always driven by what a company wants to accomplish rather than being industry-specific. BPR is effective when businesses need to break the rules and flip the script in order to achieve lofty goals. Adopting any additional process management techniques for such initiatives would be akin to rearranging the deck of a capsized ship.

Business process reengineering (BPR) is less necessary when Business process management (BPM) is effective

Employee productivity suffers significantly during process reengineering. Change is difficult to manage, and it saves a significant amount of money on analysis, reengineering, and documentation. The need for reengineering is greatly reduced if processes are managed better during runtime.
