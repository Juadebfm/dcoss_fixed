What is a Deal Structuring?

          After our business brokerage experts have thoroughly investigated all aspects of a business as part of our initial due diligence process and the client is satisfied with the results, we embark on the best possible deal structure for our clients - whether they are buying or selling. Despite the fact that several benefits can be gained or lost depending on how a deal is structured and how the negotiations go, this is arguably the least discussed aspect of buying and selling a business. Even in the brokerage industry, very few people pay attention to deal structuring for success. Before considering deal structure, a thorough understanding of the purpose and point of contact for deal sources should be considered. Deal sourcing or deal origination is a term used by finance professionals such as private equity investors and investment bankers to describe the process by which firms identify investment opportunities.
          Deal Structuring allows the buyer of a business to shape the deal to their advantage. It can result in a large transfer of value from the seller to the buyer at the closing. M&A negotiations are tense and usually involve back and forth around the price. The key to doing the right deal is buying at the right price with the right structure. With strong deal valuation and structuring skills, you can creatively frame a deal the seller will buy into without paying the highest price or the most cash at close. An expert deal structurer or management team like what we have in DCOSS, with strong experience can do this.

What are the Tax Implications of Deal Structuring

          Tax implications are generally unexpected because the type of deal structure you get when selling or buying a business is critical in shaping your tax implications. Whether the transaction is a buy-out, an asset transaction, a stock transaction, a merger, or an acquisition, the type of transaction will determine the amount of taxes you must pay. Getting the structure right is critical because a sale and purchase agreement is typically a zero-sum game - a deal structure that benefits the buyer from a tax standpoint is usually detrimental to the seller's tax situation and vice versa. That’s where our business brokerage experts at DCOSS come in.

What are the Benefits of Deal Structuring?

          You want the best deal whether you're selling or buying. When selling, a good deal structure can help you make more money from your asset. It may make the asset purchase more profitable, less risky, and far easier to operate while purchasing. Always keep in mind that, while some deal structures benefit both parties, the majority of the time, a deal structure is slanted in favor of either the buyer or the seller.

How to Structure a Deal

Selling a Company or business

          When you want to sell your company or give up equity for new funding/investments, we can help you plan a strategy that will get you the best price while still allowing you to run and grow it. We use a variety of valuation techniques to ensure that your price is appropriate for the current market conditions - neither too low nor too high - and that you have an attractive investment potential. We also assist you during the transfer period to ensure a smooth transition of ownership. Above all, we propose and negotiate the best possible deal structure for you, the seller or even in instances where you are the buyer.

          The Seller-Retained Equity structure is one example. While the seller sells the majority of their business to the buyer, they retain a portion of it. While the buyer can benefit from the seller's expertise - who created and grew the business - the seller can also reap some of the benefits of a growing business while still having a large portion of his equity liquidated into cash. A variation of this is known as In Perpetuity, which means that the seller continues to be paid by the buyer even after the buyer has paid the full price of the business. This usually occurs when the seller's skills are difficult to hire out or involve a skill set that the buyer lacks. Buyers are also comfortable with such arrangements, which keep the seller on board in perpetuity for their skills, to ensure that the business does not suffer.

Investor and Operator Collaboration or partnership

          An investor and operator partnership is a unique deal structure that can be attributed to the growth of businesses. Some entrepreneurs with experience running successful businesses but a lack of cash to make the necessary acquisitions for exponential growth can partner with investors with large cash flows. They can take the company to the next level if they work together. The operator and the investor reach an agreement in which the operator receives some equity in exchange for the capital investment.

Balancing Risks While Structuring a Deal

          Whether you're a buyer or a seller, the level of risk you're willing to accept will determine how you structure a deal. Sometimes splitting the risk in such a way that both parties get what they want is the best option.

Earn-out and victory

          A common approach is to structure a deal as an earn-out; the seller is paid over time after the buyer has purchased the business, alleviating some of the buyer's risk by not investing all of his capital at once. The buyer can use the remainder of his funds as operating capital to help grow the asset he purchased.
